1. cavity: Cavities manifest as hyperbolic reflections of varying sizes in GPR B-scan images due to the strong EM wave reflection caused by the high contrast between the air-filled voids and the surrounding material. Air's low dielectric constant amplifies the reflection intensity. Multiple reflections and diffraction patterns can arise around the cavity structures in irregular shapes, leading to an intricate backscatter profile. These features are often located beneath the surface layer and may distort the continuity of adjacent layers. Uncertainty may arise if the cavity boundaries are diffuse or if small cavities mimic noise artifacts.

2. crack: Cracks are seen as sharp and abrupt signal disruptions within otherwise continuous layers, creating localized high-intensity narrow reflections that align horizontally. These gaps act as air interfaces in the material, causing sudden waveform phase changes. The geometric cues include thin line-like features or small discontinuities in the stratification. They tend to co-appear near the surface or within the top few layers of the road structure, increasing confidence in classifying them as cracks. However, parallel cracks or heavily clustered regions might reduce clarity in isolating individual discontinuities.

3. loose: Loose zones are distinguishable by their diffuse and chaotic reflective patterns, characterized by irregular and scattered low-to-mid intensity backscatter. These result from the uneven mixture of air and soil, which disrupts wave consistency. There is no clear stratification maintained in such areas, and waveform behavior often becomes unpredictable, with minimal geometric order. Loose areas are typically more extended and less defined, making their boundaries uncertain and sometimes hard to distinguish from transitions between compact and disturbed soils.

4. normal: Normal regions exhibit consistently stratified layers with smooth, continuous reflections corresponding to undisturbed subsurface material. Waveform behavior remains predictable and orderly, with uniform amplitude and phase responses matching material strata. The absence of abrupt changes or anomalies in the geometric layering reinforces the classification of these regions as normal. Signal reflections tend to diminish uniformly with depth due to material attenuation, reducing uncertainty.

5. pipeline: Pipelines are typically identified through consistent hyperbolic reflections with sharp, high-intensity apices and symmetric tails. These are caused by EM wave reflections off the pipeline’s curved and dense material surface. Their geometric cues involve regular, smooth arcs with a distinct focus point, often beneath a uniform layer of material. Layer interaction is systematic, with overlying road stratification remaining intact but slightly bent toward the anomaly due to subsidence or material compactness caused by the pipeline's presence. However, interference from nearby objects or noise can sometimes obscure the hyperbolic pattern.
