1. cavity: In the "[Signal Amplitude]-[Reflection Strength]" view, cavities appear as areas of elevated signal amplitude and strong reflection strength. The presence of air within cavities substantially reduces the local dielectric constant, enhancing reflectivity. Multiple reflections and diffractions may be observed due to irregular shapes, potentially creating distinctive hyperbolic patterns and high-contrast interfaces as a signature trait.

2. crack: Cracks manifest as sharp, linear features with high reflection strength due to the abrupt change in material properties where air fills these gaps. The high signal amplitude is driven by the contrast between the road material and the crack cavity. This discontinuity in structure results in marked waveform anomalies and high-resolution discriminative signals.

3. loose: Loose zones are characterized by moderately elevated signal amplitude with diminished reflection strength. The chaotic arrangement of soil particles interspersed with air reduces uniformity and increases diffusivity, leading to irregular, less coherent reflective patterns. There is usually no consistent geometric signature, and waveform behavior shows variability depending on soil composition and moisture content.

4. normal: Normal areas exhibit low signal amplitude with weak reflection strength, presenting a homogenous and stable waveform profile. These are indicative of undisturbed subsurface conditions, with consistent low-contrast layers and cohesive dielectric properties, resulting in predictable and uniform signal behavior.

5. pipeline: Pipelines appear as distinctive, linear high-amplitude features with strong reflection strength due to their well-defined shape and material difference from surrounding media. The regularity and continuity of pipeline signals contrast sharply with the background, usually presenting as pronounced hyperbolic reflections with predictable periodicity across the scan, indicative of the geometric structure and consistent material composition.
