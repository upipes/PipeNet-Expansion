1. cavity: In the stratigraphic relationship of a road surface, a cavity manifests as a zone with significant discontinuity, often appearing as a distinct hyperbolic anomaly due to the strong reflections caused by the air-filled void. Layer interactions reveal anomalies interrupting the continuity of strata, suggesting voids directly beneath surface layers. These cavities can disrupt normal sedimentary alignment, which may cause wave distortions and echoes given the stark dielectric contrast between air and surrounding materials.

2. crack: Typically observed as linear or irregular horizontal anomalies within the stratigraphic setup, cracks create abrupt changes in the waveform, noticeable due to their contrast with surrounding intact areas. Layer interactions may display offsets or disrupted continuity, as cracks introduce small air gaps that dominate local reflections. The interplay of layers gives rise to sharp waveform discontinuities, reflecting the introduction of air into the previously cohesive subsurface.

3. loose: Within the stratigraphic context, loose areas appear as diffuse zones with heightened signal scatter and inconsistent reflection amplitudes, indicating disturbed or poorly compacted material. Layer interactions often present a chaotic or disrupted appearance, deviating from the expected parallel alignment. The irregular interaction between these areas and adjacent consolidated layers results in widespread scattering and variable waveform intensities, depicting the presence of increased porosity and air content.

4. normal: From a stratigraphic perspective, normal conditions show uniformly layered substrata with consistent reflection patterns. Layer interactions maintain a regular sequence with no apparent anomalies, maintaining signal continuity and stability. The waveform behavior is predictable and uniform, characterized by distinct, evenly spaced reflections corresponding to intact and homogenous material layers.

5. pipeline: In stratigraphic terms, a pipeline is identified by its distinct, consistent hyperbolic reflection pattern due to its cylindrical shape and high dielectric contrast with surrounding materials. Layer interactions often reveal pipelines as clear, patterned anomalies that cross-cut typical stratification. The reflection signature is regular and precise, with predictable curvatures indicating a metallic or PVC composition, distinguishing pipelines from surrounding earth signatures while demonstrating consistent waveform reinforcement along the pipe axis.
