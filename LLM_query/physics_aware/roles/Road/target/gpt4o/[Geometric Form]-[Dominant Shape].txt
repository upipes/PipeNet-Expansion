1. cavity: Appears as an irregular, arcuate or semi-circular void region in the subsurface, with disrupted or absent layering above it. Strong hyperbolic reflections due to the high impedance contrast between air (low dielectric constant) in the cavity and surrounding material. The hyperbolas are often sharp and well-defined in concrete and asphalt but may appear fuzzier in unpaved roads due to signal scattering from loosely packed soil.

2. crack: Detected as a slender, vertically aligned linear discontinuity. Abrupt signal reflection and phase inversion along the crack location. It manifests as localized breaks or offsets in horizontal stratigraphy. Particularly pronounced in concrete, where cracks propagate clearly, whereas in asphalt or unpaved roads, their boundaries might blur due to material heterogeneity and scattering.

3. loose: Irregular, diffuse zones without sharp boundaries, often amorphous in shape. Chaotic, weakly defined, and scattering patterns dominate the B-scan regions, with a lack of strong continuous reflections. The effect is more pronounced in unpaved roads due to naturally higher porosity but can also be identified in compacted materials (concrete/asphalt) where localized loose regions disrupt the uniform subsurface.

4. normal:  Continuous, uninterrupted parallel horizontal stratigraphy reflecting layered road structures. Reflections are uniform and consistent in amplitude within the typical depth range, with no anomalies present. Concrete exhibits well-defined, uniform layers, while asphalt may introduce weaker signal attenuation. Unpaved surfaces appear less uniform but still lack significant disturbances.

5. pipeline: Detected as hyperbolic features with a near-circular or elliptical apex in cross-section. Strong hyperbolic reflections are created due to the significant dielectric contrast between the pipeline material (often metal or plastic) and surrounding substrate. Well-pronounced hyperbolas are seen in concrete and asphalt, while in unpaved surfaces, slight diffractions at the edges may blur the feature due to soil scattering effects.
