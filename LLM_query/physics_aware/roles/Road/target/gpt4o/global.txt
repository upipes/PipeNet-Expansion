1. **cavity**: In the road domain, cavities beneath concrete, asphalt, or unpaved surfaces will appear as distinct regions with strong hyperbolic reflections due to air's low dielectric constant. The waveform within the cavity boundary often exhibits high amplitude with sharp energy diffraction at the edges, especially for irregular or rounded shapes. Signal attenuation may be minimal compared to the surrounding material, making these features highly discriminative in the B-scan. Diffractions might intensify if multiple reflections occur within the cavity. Artifacts due to interactions with nearby strata or the road surface can introduce uncertainties, particularly if cavities are small or reside near dense subsurface structures.

2. **crack**: Cracks, typically horizontal or vertically discontinuous gaps, will produce abrupt disruptions in the otherwise smooth subsurface layering in B-scans. In concrete or asphalt roads, cracks may cause localized wave reflections with relatively high amplitude, appearing as linear or erratic signal breaks. The abrupt phase changes between the crack region and intact material are distinguishing evidence. On unpaved surfaces, cracks might be less discernible due to the inherent porosity of the material, causing higher uncertainty in detecting smaller gaps. Cracks may interact with nearby anomalies, such as loose zones or cavities, complicating their identification within layered road substrates.

3. **loose**: Loose zones beneath concrete, asphalt, or unpaved surfaces manifest as areas with diffuse and chaotic signal reflections owing to the mix of air and porous soil with variable compaction. On B-scans, they are expected to appear as regions of irregular reflective intensity, lacking geometric coherence or regular layering patterns. The attenuation and scattering behavior can vary significantly based on porosity, generating spatially inconsistent signals. Loose zones are transitional in nature, sometimes blending into other anomalies such as cavities or normal layers, which can introduce interpretative ambiguity. Their detection relies upon the randomness and consistency in diffuse wave behavior, often contrasting with the crisp stratification in normal areas.

4. **normal**: Normal subsurface areas are visually characterized by clear, unbroken, and consistent stratified layers with predictable propagation and attenuation behaviors in GPR B-scans. Under concrete or asphalt surfaces, these regions exhibit smooth and parallel waveforms without abrupt reflections or scattering. On unpaved roads, normal areas may show slightly less uniform stratification due to the natural variability in soil compaction but still maintain continuity. Signal amplitude across the layers remains steady, with minimal disruptions. These areas serve as a baseline to distinguish anomalies such as cavities, cracks, and loose zones, offering high confidence in interpretation.

5. **pipeline**: Pipelines beneath road surfaces present as distinct, continuous hyperbolic anomalies in GPR B-scan images, particularly if the cross-sectional curvature aligns with the radar signal. In concrete or asphalt roads, pipelines often exhibit strong reflection signals due to the high dielectric contrast with surrounding soil or road material. On unpaved surfaces, pipelines might be harder to distinguish if surrounded by loose soil, which can scatter signal energy. Geometric cues, such as the symmetry of hyperbolas, are crucial evidence for identification. Uncertainty may arise when pipelines are buried closer to other anomalies like cavities or loose zones, where the overlapping waveforms can obscure their boundaries.
