1. cavity: In a GPR B-scan image, a cavity presents as an area of significant reflection continuity, manifesting strong signal coherence due to the stark contrast between air-filled spaces and denser surrounding materials. These cavities cause high amplitude reflections and potential diffraction patterns, indicating irregular shapes and variable depths beneath the road surface. The varying geometry might introduce slight uncertainties in exact shape delineation but the amplitude remains a reliable indicator.

2. crack: Cracks appear as localized disruptions in reflection continuity, with distinct signal incoherence at the crack site due to abrupt changes in dielectric properties. They produce clear but short linear reflections that break the flow of underlying layers in GPR images. This discontinuity is significant compared to intact road surfaces, and pattern recognition involves identifying these linear disjunctions amidst otherwise coherent subsurface layers.

3. loose: Loose subsurface areas display low reflection continuity and significant signal incoherence. The scattered reflections result from irregular, porous soil structures with varied dielectric properties, causing chaotic wave behavior. These sections lack the coherent propagation seen in denser formations, making them identifiable through erratic reflection patterns and diminished amplitude consistency.

4. normal: Normal sections maintain high reflection continuity and signal coherence, presenting consistent waveform cycles indicative of homogeneous, dense subsurface material. The GPR signal in these areas is orderly and regular, showing layers without any anomalies or disruptive objects. These segments serve as baseline references for detecting anomalies due to their stable and predictable wave behavior.

5. pipeline: Pipelines, characterized by regular reflection continuity, exhibit good signal coherence due to their uniform structure and distinct material contrast with the surrounding soil. They appear as linear features in GPR images that produce consistent reflection patterns, often delineated by geometric cues indicating cylindrical or elongated objects. Pipeline detection relies on the interpretation of these symmetric reflections, with clarity influenced by pipe material and depth.
