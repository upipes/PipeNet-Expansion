1. cavity: In GPR B-scan images of concrete, asphalt, and unpaved roads, cavities appear as high-amplitude hyperbolic reflections due to the sharp contrast between air-filled voids and the surrounding denser materials. These reflections may undergo phase changes and create multiple reverberations, especially under concrete and asphalt surfaces, showing elongation in signatures due to the uniform surface above. In unpaved roads, diffraction patterns may be more scattered, indicating irregular cavity shapes and diverse soil interactions. Detection is confident when hyperbolas are isolated and distinct.

2. crack: Cracks manifest as abrupt, thin linear reflections across the radargram, typically showing a significant drop in signal continuity in asphalt and concrete settings. The crack signature is less pronounced in unpaved surfaces due to potential backscatter from irregular grains. The waveform will exhibit sharp changes in amplitude and phase, often localized with minimal depth variation, highlighting the gap filled with air and the immediate transition back to dense material. Detections benefit from signal contrast within otherwise homogenized environments.

3. loose: In GPR images from unpaved roads, loose soils present as diffuse and chaotic regions with lower reflection amplitude than dense materials. In asphalt, loose aggregates produce irregular surface scatterings, making wave interpretation challenging. For concrete, the distinction comes from atypical diffuse signals amidst expected solid echo patterns. This class is marked by inconsistent waveforms, suggestive of non-uniform density distribution, giving uncertainty in precise boundary delineation due to mixed subsurface interactions.

4. normal: Normal regions display consistent, uniform backscatter across images of all three surface types. In concrete and asphalt, the reflections are structured and predictable, following surface geometry without anomalies. In unpaved roads, signals exhibit smoother transitions and regularity in texture. The class retains strong waveform stability and amplitude consistency, ensuring exclusion of anomalies or objects, providing reliable baselines for comparison.

5. pipeline: Pipeline signatures typically generate coherent, repetitive hyperbolic reflections, distinct under all road surfaces due to higher contrast with surrounding material. In concrete and asphalt, linearity is maintained with predictable geometric sequences, signifying uniformity and structural predictability across scans. For unpaved surfaces, pipelines might show more complex wraparound effects due to varied soil layering. Detection remains robust with visible hyperbolic peaks reflecting pipeline continuity.
