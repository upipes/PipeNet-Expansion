1. cavity: Cavities are air-filled voids found beneath road surfaces, typically irregular in shape and capable of causing structural subsidence. These anomalies produce strong reflected electromagnetic (EM) waves due to the significant contrast in dielectric constants between air and surrounding materials like soil or concrete. Multiple reflections and diffraction patterns are common as the waves interact with cavity boundaries. Waveform evidence includes pronounced hyperbolic reflections or extended ringing patterns. Discriminative geometric cues include these irregular reflection signatures contrasted against smooth, consistent layers in normal conditions. Confounding factors include the similarity of reflection patterns to those seen near air-filled pipelines or large inhomogeneities.

2. crack: Subsurface cracks are horizontal voids often filled with air within the compact underground structure of roads. These features lead to distinct wave reflections and discontinuities within the GPR B-scan, appearing as sharp horizontal lines or breaks. The waveform may abruptly change as the GPR signal encounters the air-filled gap, disrupting the otherwise smooth propagation pattern. Discriminative cues include horizontal irregularities closely parallel to the surface layer, which set them apart from air-filled cavities that have more vertical or oblique aspects. Confounding factors include differentiating shallow cracks from surface irregularities or false signals near the interface layers.

3. loose: Soil looseness beneath roads manifests in regions of higher porosity and lower density, simulating an air-soil mixture. This condition results in chaotic GPR reflections as waves scatter due to irregular and inconsistent soil structure. The waveform pattern is often diffuse and sporadic, lacking sharp, defined reflection peaks. The discriminative evidence comes from the lack of uniform waveforms; instead, the GPR image may present as a noisy and diffused background, distinctly separate from coherent reflections of pipelines or cavities. Confounders include differentiating loose soil from subsurface layers undergoing natural compaction variance or mixed material presence.

4. normal: Normal subsurface areas denote regions without anomalies, offering a consistent, undisturbed underground landscape. These regions typically exhibit uniform wave propagation with stable and predictable reflectiveness across the GPR scan. The waveform in such zones will show consistent, continuous layers without abrupt changes, reflecting regular compaction and material distribution. Discriminative cues are marked by smooth, parallel bands of reflection across scans, indicating homogeneity. Common confounders include natural material layering that might align closely but are non-anomalous when compared to other conditions.

5. pipeline: Pipelines beneath roads create consistent reflection patterns due to their regular shape and material properties. Because pipelines are typically made of metal or plastic, they contrast sharply with natural subsurface formations, generating strong discrete hyperbolic reflections on GPR scans. These reflectors show defined and repetitive waveform patterns with minimal diffraction. Geometric cues indicative of pipelines include linear or slightly arcuate signatures that can extend across several scan lines. Common confounders can arise from similar geometric shapes such as elongated cavities or large stone formations that mimic pipeline appearances but possess different dielectric properties.
